# coulomb's law:
## scalar form:
$$
\large\begin{array}{l}\\
F = k \frac{Q_1\,Q_2}{R^2}, \qquad \text{where:}\\
k = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \approx 9 \times 10^9\\
\epsilon_0 : \text{permittivity of free space}=8.854\times10^{-12} \\
\end{array}
$$
- electron charge = $1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,C$
- 1 coulomb = $6 \times 10^{18}$ electron
## vector form:
<center> <img src = "1st sem/IMAGES/fields1/Pasted image 20251103170434.png"></center>

$$
\large\vec{F_{12}} = k\frac{Q_1\,Q_2}{R_{12}^2}\,\vec{a_{12}} = \frac{Q_1\,Q_2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\,R_{12}^3} \vec{R_{12}}
$$
for n point charges acting on a charge $Q_0$ , the total force is expressed as:
$$
\large\vec{F_T} = \vec{F_{10}} + \vec{F_{20}} + \vec{F_{30}} + \cdots +\vec{F_{'n'\,0}}
$$
# electric field
- **The electric field intensity** (E) is the force per unit charge and it's unit is N/C (newton per coulomb)
$$
\large \vec{E} = \dfrac{Q}{4 \pi \epsilon_0 \, r^2}\,\vec{a_r} = \dfrac{\vec{F}}{Q_\text{charge}} \qquad \text{example:}\; \vec{E_1} = \dfrac{\vec{F_{12}}}{Q_\text{2}}
$$
- for multiple charges acting on a charge $Q_0$ , the total electric field is expressed as:
$$
\large\begin{align}
\vec{E_T} &= \vec{E_1} + \vec{E_2} + \vec{E_3} + \cdots +\vec{E_n}\\\\
& = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0}\quad\sum^n_{m=1}Q_m\,\dfrac{\vec{a_m}}{|r-r_m|^2}\\
& = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0}\quad\sum^n_{m=1}Q_m\,\dfrac{\vec{R_m}}{\;|R_m|^3}
\end{align}
$$

## Field due to --
$$
\large\begin{array}{c c c}
  \large\color{#acffcc}\text{Line Charge Distribution:} & \qquad \large\color{#acffcc}\text{Surface Charge Distribution:} \qquad & \large\color{#acffcc}\text{Volume Charge Distribution:} \\
  Q_T = \int \rho_L \, dL & Q_T = \iint \rho_S \, dS & Q_T = \iiint \rho_V \, dV
\end{array}
$$
**distribution** : has limits (finite)
same rule of *electric field*, the only difference is that Q is an integration of a function of **charge density** $\rho$

---
$$
\Large\begin{array}{l}
\color{#acffcc}\text{infinite line charge "$\rho_L$":}\quad & \vec{E} = \dfrac{\rho_L}{2 \pi \epsilon_o\,\rho}\vec{a_\rho} = \dfrac{\rho_L}{2 \pi \epsilon_o\,\rho^2}\vec{\rho} \\
\color{#acffcc}\text{infinite sheet charge "$\rho_S$":}\quad & \large\vec{E} = \dfrac{\rho_S}{2\epsilon_o}\vec{a_n}
\end{array}
$$
- $\rho_L$ is the line charge density in $C/m$ (the value will probably be given in the question)
	 same as line charge, $\rho_S$ is the sheet charge density in $C/m^2$
- $\vec{\rho}$ is the vector between the effected point and the closest point on the line
	if the line coordinates was give as $x=2 \,,\;z=-5$ , this is a line parallel to the y-axis and the closest point is ( 2 , *point y* , -5 )
- $\vec{a_n}$ is the normal vector of the surface
	the normal is the vector pointing perpendicular to the surface
- the electric field is *NOT* affected by the charge's distance from the sheet
---
$$
\Large\begin{array}{l}
\color{#acffcc}\text{ring of charge "$\rho_L$":}\quad & \vec  E = \dfrac{\rho_L ah}{2\epsilon_o (a^2 + h^2)^{3/2}} \vec a_{axis} \\
\color{#acffcc}\text{disc of charge "$\rho_S$":}\quad &\large\vec{E} = \dfrac{\rho_S}{2\epsilon_o} \left(1-\dfrac{h}{\sqrt{a^2+h^2}} \right)\,\vec a_{axis}
\end{array}
$$
- a is the ring or disc's radius, h is the charge distance from its center
-  $\vec a_{axis}$ is the ring's axis, the equation *only works* if the charge is in the ring's axis
