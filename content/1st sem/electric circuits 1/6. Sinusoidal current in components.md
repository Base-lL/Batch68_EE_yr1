## Resistor
### Formula
for: $i(t) = I_m \sin(\omega t + \theta)$ 
$$
\begin{array}{c l}
v &= iR\\
V_m \sin(\omega t + \theta) &= I_m\;R \;\sin(\omega t + \theta)\\
V_m &= I_m R
\end{array}
$$
### Phase diff.
$V$ & $I$ are both in phase, only difference is in magnitude
$\theta_v - \theta_i = 0$ 
### Power
#### instantaneous power
$$
\begin{array}{l l}
P &= v\,i\\
&= V_m \sin(\omega t + \theta)\cdot I_m \sin(\omega t + \theta)\\
&= V_m I_m \sin^2(\omega t + \theta)\\
&= \dfrac{V_m I_m}{2} \left[1-{\cos(2\omega t + 2 \theta)}\,\right]\\
\end{array}
$$
#### active power (average power)
$$
\begin{array}{l l}
P &= \dfrac{V_m I_m}{2} \left[1-\cancel{\cos(2\omega t + 2 \theta)}\,\right]\\
P &= \dfrac{V_m I_m}{2} = \dfrac{V_m}{\sqrt2} \dfrac{I_m}{\sqrt2} = VI = I^2 R = \dfrac{V^2}{R}
\end{array}
$$
the average of a sine function is Zero.
note that $V$ and $I$ are the RMS values
## Inductor
- the inductor in a DC circuit temporarily stores energy in the form of magnetic fields around the coils, due to which it opposes the current, it makes a small delay in the current to pass though
- when the voltage source is cut from the circuit, the inductor discharges all it's energy in an instant, also delaying the circuit closing
- these delays however are only present in the small time of closing and opening a circuit
### Formula
for: $i(t) = I_m \sin(\omega t + \theta)$ 
$$
\begin{array}{l l}
V &= L \dfrac{di}{dt}\\[2.5px]
&=L \dfrac{d}{dt} (I_m \sin(\omega t + \theta))\\[2.5px]
&= \omega L I_m \cos(\omega t + \theta) = V_m \cos(\omega t + \theta)\\[7.5px]
V_m &= I_m \omega L
\end{array}
$$
$$
\because  \omega L = X_L \quad \therefore V_m = I_m X_L \;,\quad V = \omega L I
$$
### Phase diff.
- in an AC circuit, due to the building and collapsing of magnetic fields, it results in constant delays in current with the voltage **which is why:**
$i_L$ lags $v_L$ by 90 degrees *OR* $v_L$ leads $i_L$ by 90 degrees.
$\theta_v - \theta_i = 90\degree = \dfrac \pi 2 \text{rad}$ 
### Power 
#### Instantaneous power
$$
\begin{array}{l l}
P &= v\,i\\
&= V_m \cos(\omega t + \theta)\cdot I_m \sin(\omega t + \theta)\\
&= V_m I_m \cos(\omega t + \theta)\sin(\omega t + \theta)\\
&= \dfrac{V_m I_m}{2} \sin(2\omega t + 2\theta)\\
\end{array}
$$
#### Active power (average power)
$$
P = \dfrac{V_m I_m}{2} \cancel{\sin(2\omega t + 2\theta)} = \text{ZERO}
$$
the inductor doesn't absorb active power, it stores some then returns them
#### Reactive power
$$
\begin{array}{l l}
Q = \dfrac{V_m I_m}{2} = VI = I^2X_L = \dfrac{V^2}{X_L}
\end{array}
$$
### Stored energy
$$
W = \int P dt = \int vi\,dt = \int L \dfrac{di}{dt}\,i\, dt = L\int i \,di
$$
$$
W = \dfrac12 L\,i^2 = \dfrac12 L\,I_m^2 \sin^2(\omega t + \theta) = \dfrac14 L\,I_m^2 [\,1-\cos(2\omega t + 2\theta)\,]
$$
#### max energy at $\cos(2\omega t + 2\theta) = -1$ 
$$
W_\text{max} = \dfrac12 L\,I_m^2
$$
## Capacitor
- the capacitor in DC circuit stores energy, after reaching max energy it acts as an open switch, preventing further current to pass through
- when the voltage source is cut from the circuit, the capacitor discharges the energy it stored, acting as a temporarily source until all power is lost from it
### Formula
for: $v(t) = V_m \sin(\omega t + \theta)$ 
$$
\begin{array}{l l}
i &= C \dfrac{dv}{dt}\\
&= C \dfrac{d}{dt}(V_m \sin(\omega t + \theta)) \\
&= \omega\,C V_m \cos(\omega t + \theta) = I_m \cos(\omega t + \theta)\\
I_m &= \omega\,C V_m
\end{array}
$$
$$
\because I_m  = \dfrac{V_m}{1/\omega C} \quad \therefore  -\dfrac{1}{\omega C} = X_C \;,\quad I = \omega C V
$$
it is assumed that the capacitance is $X_C$ is negative
### Phase diff.
- in an AC circuit, due to the rapid charge and discharge, it results in constant delays in voltage with the current **which is why:**
$v_L$ lags $i_L$ by 90 degrees. *OR* $i_L$ leads $v_L$ by 90 degrees
$\theta_v - \theta_i = -90\degree = -\dfrac \pi 2 \text{rad}$ 
### Power 
#### instantaneous power
$$
\begin{array}{l l}
P &= v\,i\\
&= V_m \sin(\omega t + \theta)\cdot I_m \cos(\omega t + \theta)\\
&= V_m I_m \sin(\omega t + \theta)\cos(\omega t + \theta)\\
&= \dfrac{V_m I_m}{2} \sin(2\omega t + 2\theta)\\
\end{array}
$$

#### active power (average power)
$$
P = \dfrac{V_m I_m}{2} \cancel{\sin(2\omega t + 2\theta)} = \text{ZERO}
$$
the capacitor -much like the inductor- doesn't absorb active power
#### Reactive power
$$
\begin{array}{l l}
Q = \dfrac{V_m I_m}{2} = VI = I^2X_C = \dfrac{V^2}{X_C}
\end{array}
$$
### Stored energy
$$
W = \int P dt = \int vi\,dt = \int  v\,C \dfrac{dv}{dt}\, dt = C\int v \,dv
$$
$$
W = \dfrac12 C\,v^2 = \dfrac12 C\,V_m^2 \sin^2(\omega t + \theta) = \dfrac14 C\,V_m^2 [\,1-\cos(2\omega t + 2\theta)\,]
$$
#### max energy at $\cos(2\omega t + 2\theta) = -1$ 
$$
W_\text{max} = \dfrac12 C\,V_m^2
$$


# RLC circuit
## components voltages


the total current in the circuit $v=v_R +  v_L +  v_C$
where:
$$
\begin{array}{l l}
v_R = R\;I_m\sin(\omega t + \theta)\\
v_L = \omega L\;I_m\sin(\omega t + \theta + \dfrac{\pi}{2})\\
v_r = \dfrac{1}{\omega C}\;I_m\sin(\omega t + \theta - \dfrac{\pi}{2})
= -\dfrac{1}{\omega C}\;I_m\sin(\omega t + \theta + \dfrac{\pi}{2})
\end{array}
$$
substituting in $v$ : 
$$
R\;I_m\sin(\omega t + \theta) + \omega L\;I_m\sin(\omega t + \theta + \dfrac{\pi}{2})-\dfrac{1}{\omega C}\;I_m\sin(\omega t + \theta + \dfrac{\pi}{2})
$$
$$
I_m \left[ R \sin(\omega t + \theta) + \left(  \omega L - \dfrac{1}{\omega C}\right)\sin(\omega t + \theta + \dfrac{\pi}{2}) \right]
$$
$$
I_m \left[ R \sin(\omega t + \theta) + X \cos(\omega t + \theta) \right]
$$
where $X = X_L + X_C = \omega L - \dfrac{1}{\omega C}$
$$
v = I_m \sqrt{R^2 + X^2} \sin(\omega t + \theta + \phi)
$$
$$
\begin{array}{r l} V&= I_m Z \sin(\omega t + \theta + \phi)\\
&= V_m \sin(\omega t + \theta + \phi)
\end{array}
$$
where:
$$
Z = \sqrt{R^2 + X^2} = \sqrt{R^2 + \left(  \omega L - \dfrac{1}{\omega C}\right)^2}
$$
$$
\phi = \tan^{-1}\left(\dfrac X R\right) \;,\quad R = Z \cos\phi \;,\quad X=Z\sin\phi
$$
