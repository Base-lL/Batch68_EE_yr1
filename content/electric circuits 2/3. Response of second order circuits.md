there will be 4 types of responses for the RLC circuit:

- **parallel** circuit _natural_ response
- **series** circuit _step_ response

# The Natural Response of a Parallel RLC Circuit

![[Pasted image 20260413165618.png]]

solved by getting the current equation where:
$$
i_C + i_L + i_R = 0
$$
substituting in the current value for each component:
$$
\left[ \frac{v}{R} \right] + \left[ \frac{1}{L} \int^t_0 v\,dt + I_0 \right] + \left[C \frac{dv}{dt} \right] = 0
$$
integrate each side with respect to $dt$ :
$$
\frac{1}{R} \frac{dv}{dt} + \frac{v}{L} + C\frac{dv^2}{d^2t} = 0
$$
divide each side by $C$ and rearrange:
$$
\frac{d^2v}{dt^2} + \frac{1}{RC} \frac{dv}{dt} + \frac{v}{LC}  = 0
$$
we get a [[4. معادلات تفاضلية خطية ذات معاملات ثابتة|linear differential equation with constant coefficients]]. a quick reminder, we used to get 2 solutions:
- **Complementary** solution المكمل
- **Particular** solution الخاص
the general solution is the sum of the 2, but there is no **Particular** solution since the main equation equals doesn't have a $t$ term

## The general solution
assume that $v = A e^{st}$
substitute that in the main equation and divide by $e^{st}$
$$
s^2 + \frac{s}{RC} + \frac{1}{LC} = 0
$$
solving the polynomial gives 2 values where:
$$
s_{1,2} = -\frac{1}{2RC} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{2RC}\right)^2 - \frac{1}{LC}}
$$
by replacing the values as shown:
- $\alpha = \dfrac{1}{2RC}$ and is called the **Neper** Frequency
- $\omega_o = \dfrac{1}{LC}$ and is called the **Resonant Radian** Frequency

substitute back in $s_{1,2}$
$$
s_{1,2} = -\alpha \pm \sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega_0^2}
$$
this leaves us 3 possible responses:

### Over damped Response: If $\alpha > \omega_0$
the roots of the characteristic equation will be *pure negative* **real** numbers

the **equation for voltage** becomes:
$$
v(t) = A_1 e^{s_1t} + A_2 e^{s_2t}
$$
to get the constants, use the following equations:
$$
v(0^+) = A_1 + A_2
$$

$$
\frac{dv(0^+)}{dt} = \frac{i_c(0^+)}{C} = s_1A_1 + s_2A_1
$$

### Under damped Response: If $\alpha < \omega_0$
the roots of the characteristic equation will be *conjugate* **complex** numbers

*for ease* the roots become of the complex form:
$$
s_{1,2} = -\alpha \pm j \omega_d \quad,\quad \omega_d =  \sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega_0^2}
$$
where the new term $\omega_d$ is the **damped radian** frequency

the **equation for voltage** becomes:
$$
v(t) = B_1 e^{-\alpha t} \cos\omega_d t + B_2 e^{-\alpha t} \sin\omega_d t
$$
to get the constants, use the following equations:
$$
v(0^+) = V_0 = B_1
$$

$$
\frac{dv(0^+)}{dt} = \frac{i_c(0^+)}{C} = -\alpha B_1 + \omega_d B_2
$$

### Critical Response: If $\alpha = \omega_0$
the roots of the characteristic equation will be *equal negative* **real** numbers

the **equation for voltage** becomes:
$$
v(t) = D_1 t e^{-\alpha t} + D_2 e^{-\alpha t}
$$
to get the constants, use the following equations:
$$
v(0^+) = V_0 = D_2
$$

$$
\frac{dv(0^+)}{dt} = \frac{i_c(0^+)}{C} = D_1 - \alpha D_2
$$

*NOTE:* there is no difference between $D_1$ and $D_2$ so it is safe to switch between the 2 as long as you do the same in all the formulas


# The Step Response of a Series RLC Circuit

![[Pasted image 20260413215044.png]]

by applying kirchoff's voltage law:
$$
v_r + v_L + v_C = V
$$
substituting in the voltage values for each component:
$$
Ri + L\frac{di}{dt} + v_c = V
$$
using the capacitor current equation: 
$$
i = C \dfrac{dv_c}{dt} \; \xrightarrow{\text{integrate by $dt$}} \; \frac{di}{dt} = C \frac{d^2 v_c}{dt^2}
$$
substituting in the main equation and dividing by $LC$ :
$$
\frac{d^2 v_c}{dt^2} + \frac{R}{L} \frac{dv_c}{dt} + \frac{v_c}{LC} = \frac{V}{LC}
$$
we get an equation similar to the one we had above

## General solution
the same 3 cases apply here
$$
\begin{array}{l}
v(t) = A_1 e^{s_1t} + A_2 e^{s_2t} & \text{: over damped} \\
v(t) = B_1 e^{-\alpha t} \cos\omega_d t + B_2 e^{-\alpha t} \sin\omega_d t & \text{: under damped} \\
v(t) = D_1 t e^{-\alpha t} + D_2 e^{-\alpha t} & \text{: critically damped}
\end{array}
$$
and they're solved the same way as above

*NOTE:* $\alpha$ here will have a different value
$$
\alpha = \frac{R}{2L}
$$
