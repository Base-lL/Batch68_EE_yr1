a there phase circuit consists of 3 connected voltage sources that:
- have the same **amplitude** and **frequency**
- all have a phase difference of 120°

there is 2 possible types for three phase:
$$
\begin{array}{l|l}
\text{abc - postive} & \text{acb - negative}\\[5px]
\hline
V_a = V_m \angle 0\degree & V_a = V_m \angle 0\degree \\[5px]
V_b = V_m \angle{-120}\degree & V_b = V_m \angle{+120}\degree \\[5px]
V_c = V_m \angle{+120}\degree & V_c = V_m \angle{-120}\degree \\
\end{array}
$$

with 2 possible connections, **Wye** and **Delta**

# Analysis of the Wye-Wye Circuit

![[Pasted image 20260417170235.png]]

the impedance distribution is as follow:
- $Z_{ga}\,,Z_{gb}\,,Z_{gc}$ : the impedance of the source
- $Z_{1a}\,,Z_{1b}\,,Z_{1c}$ : the impedance of the line
- $Z_{A}\;,Z_{B}\;,Z_{C}\,$ : the impedance of the load

for the voltage nodes:
- $V_N$ is the load's node voltage
- $V_n$ is the source's node voltage

but for now, let $V_N$ be the voltage between the 2 nodes

the node voltage equation is:
$$
\frac{V_N}{Z_0} + \frac{V_N - V_{a'n}}{Z_A + Z_{1a} + Z_{ga}} + \frac{V_N - V_{b'n}}{Z_B + Z_{1b} + Z_{gb}} + \frac{V_N - V_{c'n}}{Z_C + Z_{1c} + Z_{gc}} = 0
$$

## Balanced 3 phase circuit

for a 3 phase circuit to be balanced:
- $V_{a'n}\,,V_{b'n}\,,V_{c'n}$ are a set of balanced three-phase voltages.
- $Z_{ga}=Z_{gb}=Z_{gc}$
- $Z_{1a}=Z_{1b}=Z_{1c}$
- $Z_{A}=Z_{B}=Z_{C}$

this shortens the previous equation into:
$$
V_N \left( \frac{1}{Z_0} + \frac{3}{Z_\phi} \right) = \frac{V_{a'n}+V_{b'n}+V_{c'n}}{Z_\phi}
$$
where:
$$
Z_\phi\; 
\begin{array}{l}
=Z_A + Z_{1a} + Z_{ga}\\
=Z_B + Z_{1b} + Z_{gb}\\
=Z_C + Z_{1c} + Z_{gc}
\end{array}
$$


and since the **right-hand-side** has to be *equal to zero*: $V_N$ *has to equal zero*
this means the voltage across the source and load nodes is equal, so *no current $I_N$ passes through*

the neutral wire can be removed or replaced with a short circuit between the neutral nodes

## Line current

$$
I_{aA} = \frac{V_{a'n} - V_N}{Z_A + Z_{1a} + Z_{ga}} = \frac{V_{a'n}}{Z_\phi}
$$

$$
I_{bB} = \frac{V_{b'n} - V_N}{Z_B + Z_{1b} + Z_{gb}} = \frac{V_{b'n}}{Z_\phi}
$$

$$
I_{cC} = \frac{V_{c'n} - V_N}{Z_C + Z_{1c} + Z_{gc}} = \frac{V_{c'n}}{Z_\phi}
$$


## line-to-line voltage
![[Pasted image 20260417175727.png]]

for simplicity we call it the **line voltage**
as for the line-to-neutral, we call it the **phasor voltage**

the 3 line voltages:
$$
\begin{array}{l}
V_{AB} = V_{AN} - V_{BN} \\
V_{BC} = V_{CN} - V_{CN} \\
V_{AB} = V_{BN} - V_{AN} \\
\end{array}
$$

assuming a positive sequence (abc):
$$
\begin{array}{l}
V_{AN} = V_\phi \, \angle 0\degree \\
V_{BN} = V_\phi \, \angle{-120}\degree \\
V_{CN} = V_\phi \, \angle{+120}\degree 
\end{array}
$$

substituting that in:
$$
\begin{array}{l}
V_{AB} = \sqrt3 \, V_\phi \, \angle{30}\degree \\
V_{BC} = \sqrt3 \, V_\phi \, \angle{-90}\degree \\
V_{CA} = \sqrt3 \, V_\phi \, \angle{
150}\degree 
\end{array}
$$

the line voltage for the phasor voltage is:
- $\sqrt3$ times the magnitude
- the line voltage leads by 30°

# Analysis of the Wye-Delta Circuit 

![[Pasted image 20260417212133.png]]

for most cases where there is a delta connection *(also including the source)*, it is solved by turning it into a wye connection
for the case of the supply load, [[3. Some Methods for Solution of Electric Circuits#star / delta connections|refer to this for the transformation equations]]
for the case of a balanced load, transforming from delta to wye:
$$
Z_Y = \frac{Z_\Delta}{3}
$$
for the 3 currents:
$$
\begin{array}{l}
I_{aA} = \sqrt3 \, I_\phi \, \angle{-30}\degree \\
I_{bB} = \sqrt3 \, I_\phi \, \angle{-150}\degree \\
I_{cC} = \sqrt3 \, I_\phi \, \angle{90}\degree 
\end{array}
$$
where $I_\phi$ is the magnitude of any of the phasor currents

# Analysis of the Delta-Delta Circuit

V_{ab} = V_{AB}\;,\quad V_{bc} = V_{BC}\;,\quad V_{ca} = V_{CA}

## The circuit has line impedance

for this case the voltage generator and the load impedance has to turn into a Wye-Wye circuit

$$
\begin{array}{l}
V_{an} = \frac{V_p}{\sqrt3}\angle{-30\degree}\\

V_{cn} = \frac{V_p}{\sqrt3}\angle{90\degree}

$$

# Power Calculations

![[Pasted image 20260418003209.png]]

## the average power
the average power delivered to each phasor:
$$
\begin{array}{l}
P_A = |V_{AN}|\,|I_{aA}|\cos(\theta_{vA} - \theta_{iA}) \\[3px]
P_B = |V_{BN}|\,|I_{bB}|\cos(\theta_{vB} - \theta_{iB}) \\[3px]
P_C = |V_{CN}|\,|I_{cC}|\cos(\theta_{vC} - \theta_{iC}) \\
\end{array}
$$
for a balanced system:
$$
\begin{array}{l}
P_A \\ P_B \\ P_C
\end{array}
= P_\phi =  V_\phi I_\phi \cos(\theta_\phi)
$$
the total average power:
$$
P_T = 3 P_\phi = \sqrt3 V_L I_L \cos\theta_\phi
$$
the power is measured in **Watts**

the power factor *(or called pf lead/lag in questions)* is the cosine part of the power: $\cos\theta_\phi$
you can get the angle $\theta_\phi$ from it 
$\sin\theta_\phi$ can be found by using: $\sqrt{1 - \cos^2\theta_\phi}$

a pf lag will result in a positive Complex Power
a pf lead will result in a negative Complex Power

## Complex Power
same equations apply with the difference being that its $\sin$ not $\cos$:
$$
Q_\phi =  V_\phi I_\phi \sin(\theta_\phi)
$$

$$
Q_T = 3Q_\phi = \sqrt3 V_L I_L \sin\theta_\phi
$$
the active power is measured in **VAR** : voltage ampere reactive
## Total power

$$
S_\phi = p_\phi + jQ_\phi = V_\phi I_\phi
$$

$$
S_T = 3S_\phi = \sqrt3 V_L I_L \angle{\theta_\phi}
$$

# The Two-Wattmeter Method
![[Pasted image 20260418005325.png]]

this works *only* on a balanced circuit, since we can take line bB as a reference.
for a non balanced circuit, the neutral wire is taken as reference so we'd need 3 watt-meters

cc stands for current coil
pc stand for potential coil
$$
W_1 = |V_{AB}|\,|I_{aA}|\cos(\theta_\phi + 30\degree)
$$

$$
W_2 = |V_{CB}|\,|I_{cC}|\cos(\theta_\phi - 30\degree)
$$
where $\theta_\phi$ is the **impedance** phase angle

the total power is the sum of the 2 readings: $P_T = W_1 + W_2$
