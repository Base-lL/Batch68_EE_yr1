a there phase circuit consists of 3 connected voltage sources that:
- have the same **amplitude** and **frequency**
- all have a phase difference of 120°

there is 2 possible types for three phase:
$$
\begin{array}{l|l}
\text{abc - postive} & \text{acb - negative}\\[5px]
\hline
V_a = V_m \angle 0\degree & V_a = V_m \angle 0\degree \\[5px]
V_b = V_m \angle{-120}\degree & V_b = V_m \angle{+120}\degree \\[5px]
V_c = V_m \angle{+120}\degree & V_c = V_m \angle{-120}\degree \\
\end{array}
$$

with 2 possible connections, **Wye** and **Delta**

# Analysis of the Wye-Wye Circuit

![[Pasted image 20260417170235.png]]

the impedance distribution is as follow:
- $Z_{ga}\,,Z_{gb}\,,Z_{gc}$ : the impedance of the source
- $Z_{1a}\,,Z_{1b}\,,Z_{1c}$ : the impedance of the line
- $Z_{A}\;,Z_{B}\;,Z_{C}\,$ : the impedance of the load

for the voltage nodes:
- $V_N$ is the load's node voltage
- $V_n$ is the source's node voltage

but for now, let $V_N$ be the voltage between the 2 nodes

the node voltage equation is:
$$
\frac{V_N}{Z_0} + \frac{V_N - V_{a'n}}{Z_A + Z_{1a} + Z_{ga}} + \frac{V_N - V_{b'n}}{Z_B + Z_{1b} + Z_{gb}} + \frac{V_N - V_{c'n}}{Z_C + Z_{1c} + Z_{gc}} = 0
$$

## Balanced 3 phase circuit

for a 3 phase circuit to be balanced:
- $V_{a'n}\,,V_{b'n}\,,V_{c'n}$ are a set of balanced three-phase voltages.
- $Z_{ga}=Z_{gb}=Z_{gc}$
- $Z_{1a}=Z_{1b}=Z_{1c}$
- $Z_{A}=Z_{B}=Z_{C}$

this shortens the previous equation into:
$$
V_N \left( \frac{1}{Z_0} + \frac{3}{Z_\phi} \right) = \frac{V_{a'n}+V_{b'n}+V_{c'n}}{Z_\phi}
$$
where:
$$
Z_\phi\; 
\begin{array}{l}
=Z_A + Z_{1a} + Z_{ga}\\
=Z_B + Z_{1b} + Z_{gb}\\
=Z_C + Z_{1c} + Z_{gc}
\end{array}
$$


and since the **right-hand-side** has to be *equal to zero*: $V_N$ *has to equal zero*
this means the voltage across the source and load nodes is equal, so *no current $I_N$ passes through*

the neutral wire can be removed or replaced with a short circuit between the neutral nodes

## Line current

$$
I_{aA} = \frac{V_{a'n} - V_N}{Z_A + Z_{1a} + Z_{ga}} = \frac{V_{a'n}}{Z_\phi}
$$

$$
I_{bB} = \frac{V_{b'n} - V_N}{Z_B + Z_{1b} + Z_{gb}} = \frac{V_{b'n}}{Z_\phi}
$$

$$
I_{cC} = \frac{V_{c'n} - V_N}{Z_C + Z_{1c} + Z_{gc}} = \frac{V_{c'n}}{Z_\phi}
$$


## line-to-line voltage
![[Pasted image 20260417175727.png]]

for simplicity we call it the **line voltage**
as for the line-to-neutral, we call it the **phasor voltage**

the 3 line voltages:
$$
\begin{array}{l}
V_{AB} = V_{AN} - V_{BN} \\
V_{BC} = V_{CN} - V_{CN} \\
V_{AB} = V_{BN} - V_{AN} \\
\end{array}
$$

assuming a positive sequence (abc):
$$
\begin{array}{l}
V_{AN} = V_\phi \, \angle 0\degree \\
V_{BN} = V_\phi \, \angle{-120}\degree \\
V_{CN} = V_\phi \, \angle{+120}\degree 
\end{array}
$$

substituting that in:
$$
\begin{array}{l}
V_{AB} = \sqrt3 \, V_\phi \, \angle{30}\degree \\
V_{BC} = \sqrt3 \, V_\phi \, \angle{-90}\degree \\
V_{CA} = \sqrt3 \, V_\phi \, \angle{-120}\degree 
\end{array}
$$

the line voltage for the phasor voltage is:
- $\sqrt3$ times the magnitude
- the line voltage leads by 30°
