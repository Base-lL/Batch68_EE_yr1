# work done on a volume distribution
$$
W_E = \frac12 \int_{VOL} (\vec D \cdot \vec E)\;dv = \frac12 \int_{VOL} (\epsilon_0 E^2)\;dv
$$
# Deriving an expression for work

## point charges

we initially have a volume distribution $\Delta v$ that is empty of charges.
we want an expression for the energy required to bring N point charges from infinity to any point $P$ within the volume.

1. charge $Q_1$: it takes no work to transfer it to $P_1$ *(remember the volume was charge free)* $W = 0$
2. charge $Q_2$: it takes $W = Q_2 V_{21}$ to transfer it to $P_2$
3. charge $Q_3$: it takes $W = Q_3 V_{21} + Q_3 V_{23}$ to transfer it to $P_3$

and so on, with every charge that enters, we have to calculate the potential difference between it and the other points.

---
lets just focus on 3 point charges

$$
\begin{array}{l l}
W_E &= W_1 + W_2 + W_3\\
&= 0 + Q_2 V_{21} + Q_3 (V_{31} + V_{32}) \qquad (1)
\end{array}
$$
---
counting the charges in reverse, notice that order doesn't matter
$$
\begin{array}{l l}
W_E &= W_3 + W_2 + W_1\\
&= 0 + Q_2 V_{21} + Q_1 (V_{12} + V_{13})  \qquad (2)
\end{array}
$$
---
from (1) and (2):
$$
\begin{array}{r l}
2\,W_E &= Q_1 (V_{12} + V_{13}) + Q_2 (V_{21} + V_{23}) + Q_3 (V_{31} + V_{32})\\
&= Q_1 V_1 + Q_2 V_2 + Q_3 V_3\\[5px]
W_E &= \dfrac12 (Q_1 V_1 + Q_2 V_2 + Q_3 V_3)
\end{array}
$$
---
more generally, **for N points:**
$$
W_E = \dfrac12 \sum^n_{k=1} Q_k V_k
$$

## volume charges
instead of point charges, the volume was a continuous charge distribution $\rho_v$ then:
$$
W_E = \frac{1}{2} \int_{VOL} \rho_v V \, dv
$$
we already know that $\rho_v = \vec{\nabla} \cdot \vec{D}$ so we can substitute it it:
$$
W_E = \frac{1}{2} \int_{VOL} (\vec{\nabla} \cdot \vec{D}) V \, dv
$$
---
**important:** this is new, we can use some vector analysis to get this:
$$
\because \vec{\nabla} \cdot (V \vec{D}) = (\vec{\nabla} \cdot \vec{D}) V + \vec{D} \cdot \vec{\nabla} V
$$
which is like the chain rule for derivatives, note that all quantities are *scalar* since it's the dot product of 2 vectors.
we can rearrange them to get:
$$
\therefore (\vec{\nabla} \cdot \vec{D}) V = \vec{\nabla} \cdot (V \vec{D}) - \vec{D} \cdot \vec{\nabla} V \\
$$
---
substituting back in out equation:
$$
W_E = \frac{1}{2} \int_{VOL} (\vec{\nabla} \cdot [V\vec{D}]) \, dv - \frac{1}{2} \int_{VOL} [\vec{D} \cdot \vec{\nabla} V] \, dv 
$$
applying the **Divergence theorem** to the first term:
$$
W_E = \cancel{\frac{1}{2} \oint (V\vec{D}) \cdot d\vec{s}} - \frac{1}{2} \int_{VOL} (\vec{D} \cdot \vec{\nabla} V) \, dv
$$
the first term is *equal to zero* ==(insert reasons)== and we can substitute $\vec E = -\nabla V$ to the second term
$$
W_E = -\frac12 \int_{VOL} (\vec D \cdot -\vec E)\;dv = \frac12 \int_{VOL} (\epsilon_0 E^2)\;dv
$$
