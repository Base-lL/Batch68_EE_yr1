# Current and Current Density
by definition, current density is the amount of current flowing through a unit area of a conductor

$$
I = \iint \vec{J} \cdot d\vec{S} \quad [A]
$$

## For uniform cross section area:

$$
J = \frac{I}{A} \quad [A/m^2]
$$
---
# Convection Current Density

$$
J = \rho_v V \quad [A/m^2]
$$

* $\rho_v$ : Volume charge density $[C/m^3]$
* $V$ : Drift velocity $[m/s]$

## how to derive
$$
\because J = \frac{I}{A} \;,\quad \Delta I = \dfrac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}
$$
we can substitute $\Delta Q$ as follows
$$
\Delta Q = \rho_v\,\Delta v
$$
and for a wire section, it has:
- cross section area $\Delta S$
-  length $\Delta L$

if we move the wire a distance $\Delta X$,we get 
$$
\Delta Q = \rho_v\,\Delta S \Delta x
$$
substitute back to the current equation:
$$
\Delta I = \rho_v\,\Delta S \dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}
$$
and we know that $\because V_x = \dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}$:
$$
\Delta I = \rho_v\,\Delta S\,V_x 
$$
substitute in current density equation:
$$
\dfrac{\Delta I}{\Delta S} = J_x = \rho_v V_x
$$
and so we get the general equation above

# Point Form of Ohm's Law

$$
J = \sigma E \quad [A/m^2]
$$

$$
V_e = -\mu_e E \quad [m/s]
$$

## Electron Mobility ($\mu_e$):

$$
\mu_e = \frac{e T}{m}
$$

* $e$ : Electron charge $\approx -1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, C$
* $T$ : Average time between any two collisions
* $m$ : Electron mass $\approx 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, kg$

## Conductivity ($\sigma$)

$$
\sigma = -\rho_e \mu_e = \frac{n e^2 T}{m} \quad [\text{Siemens}]
$$

where $\rho_e$ is the electron volume charge density $[C/m^3]$

## how to derive
$$
\because F = ma \;,\quad a = \dfrac V t
$$
**Note** since the electrons aren't in free space and are constantly colliding. $t$ is the *average collision time* between 2 electrons

the force applied on an electron that has charge $-e$ is $f = -eE$
$$
\dfrac{mV}{t} = -e E \quad \rightarrow \quad V = -\frac{e T}{m} E
$$
substitute with **electron mobility $\mu_e$** , we *get*  $V = -\mu_e E$

if there is "n" electrons per unit volume, then $\rho_v = - n e$
substituting in the **Convection Current Density** equation:
$$
J = \rho_v V = -ne \cdot -\frac{e T}{m}E = \frac{n e^2 T}{m}E
$$
substituting with **Conductivity $\sigma$** , we *get* $J = \sigma E$

# Continuity Equation
$$
\vec \nabla \cdot \vec{J} = -\frac{\partial \rho_v}{\partial t}
$$
## how to derive
$$
\because I = \oint \vec J \cdot \vec{dS} = - \dfrac{dQ}{dt}
$$
applying divergence theorem:
$$
\int_{\text{VOL}} (\vec \nabla \cdot \vec J )\,dv = - \dfrac{dQ}{dt}
$$
$$
\because Q = \int_{\text{VOL}} \rho_v\,dv
$$
$$
\therefore - \dfrac{dQ}{dt} = - \dfrac{d}{dt} \int_{\text{VOL}} \rho_v\,dv
$$
substituting back to the above equation:
$$
\int_{\text{VOL}} (\vec \nabla \cdot \vec J )\,dv = - \dfrac{d}{dt} \int_{\text{VOL}} \rho_v\,dv
$$
removing the integration from both sides (technically)
$$
\nabla \cdot \vec{J} = -\frac{d \rho_v}{d t}
$$
we can turn the derivative into a partial derivative to get the main equation

# Power Dissipated

$$
P = \iiint (\vec{E} \cdot \vec{J}) \, dv
$$

$$
P = V I = I^2 R
$$

$$
\vec{E} \cdot \vec{J} = \sigma |E|^2
$$

## Power density:

$$
w_p = \frac{dP}{dv} = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{J} = \sigma |E|^2
$$

---

# For any conductor with uniform cross section area:

$$
E = \frac{V}{L}
$$

## The Resistance:

$$
R = \frac{L}{\sigma A} = \frac{\rho L}{A}
$$

* resistivity: $\rho$  $= \dfrac{1}{\sigma} \quad [\Omega \cdot m]$

---

# For non uniform cross section:

$$
R = \frac{V}{I} = \frac{\int \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{L}}{\oint \vec{J} \cdot d\vec{S}}
$$
