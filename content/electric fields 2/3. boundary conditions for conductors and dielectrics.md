similar to how light refracts when passing through different mediums, the same idea applies to electric fields and it applies at the *boundary surface*

# Conductors
for a conducting material:
- there isn't a **charge** nor an **electric field** *inside it*
- the **charge** lies only at the conducting surface $\rho_s$ 
- the **electric field** at the conductor surface is *always normal to the surface*
$$
\vec E = \vec {E_n} \quad,\quad \vec D = \vec {D_n} 
$$
- the **tangential** component of the electric field is *ZERO*
$$
\vec{E_t} = \vec{D_t} = 0
$$
- the **normal flux density** is equal to the *surface charge density*
$$
\vec{D_n} = \rho_s \quad , \quad \vec{E_n} = \dfrac{\rho_s}{\epsilon_0}
$$
- the conductor is an **equipotential surface**

### Tangential component
assume a small rectangle loop $abcd$ with height $\Delta h$ and width $\Delta L$ 
- the work done on the loop is zero so: $\oint \vec E \cdot \vec{dL} = 0$ 
- substituting in the loop sides:
$$
\int^b_a \vec{E_t} \cdot \vec{dL} + \int^c_b \vec{E_n} \cdot \vec{dL} + \int^d_c \vec{E_t} \cdot \vec{dL} + \int^a_d \vec{E_n} \cdot \vec{dL} = 0
$$
- as $\Delta h$ approaches zero, the distance cb and ad also approach zero, cancelling the terms

$$
\int^b_a \vec{E_t} \cdot \vec{dL} + \int^d_c \vec{E_t} \cdot \vec{dL} = 0
$$
- since side dc is inside the conductor, it contains no charge, leaving us with
$$
\int^b_a \vec{E_t} \cdot \vec{dL} = 0
$$
- as $\Delta h$ approaches zero, this can be generalized more into $E_t \cdot \Delta L = 0$ . since $\Delta L$ *approaches but never becomes zero* 
$$
E_t = D_t = 0
$$
### Normal component
assume a cylinder with height $\Delta h$ and faces of area $\Delta S$ 
- from **Gauss law**:  $\oint \vec D \cdot \vec{ds} = Q_{en}$  , substituting in the faces
$$
\oint_{\text{top}} D_n\,ds + \oint_{\text{side}} D_t\,ds + \oint_{\text{bottom}} D_n\,ds = \int_{s} \rho_s\,ds
$$
and due to the same reasons as tangential component:
- as $\Delta h$ approaches zero, the side integral approaches zero
- since the bottom face is inside the conductor, it has no charge

that leaves us with the general formula
$$
D_n\Delta S = \rho_s \Delta S
$$
and we get
$$
\vec{D_n} = \rho_s \quad , \quad \vec{E_n} = \dfrac{\rho_s}{\epsilon_0}
$$
# Dielectrics
the tangential components of the *Electric field* are equal
$$
\dfrac{D_{t1}}{\epsilon_{r1}} = E_{t1} = E_{t2} = \dfrac{D_{t2}}{\epsilon_{r2}}
$$
the normal components of the *Electric flux density* are equal
$$
D_{n1} = D_{n2} \;,\qquad \epsilon_{r1} E_{n1} = \epsilon_{r2} E_{n2}
$$
the angle between the $D$ (or $E$) and the normal to the surface generally:
$$
\theta = \arctan\dfrac{\text{Tangential component}}{\text{normal component}}
$$

*to derive expressions, use the same steps as the conductor without discarding the last term*


![[Pasted image 20260329175525.png]]
for the cases of $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ (*$D_t$ is the same as $D_{\text{tan}}$* ) :
$$
\theta_1 = \arctan\frac{E_{t1}}{E_{n1}} = \arctan\frac{D_{t1}}{D_{n1}} \quad,\quad \theta_2 = \arctan\frac{E_{t2}}{E_{n2}} = \arctan\frac{D_{t2}}{D_{n2}}
$$
the ratio of the tangential components gives:
$$
\frac{D_{t1}}{D_{t2}} = \frac{D_1 \sin \theta_1}{D_2 \sin \theta_2} = \frac{\epsilon_1}{\epsilon_2}
$$
# polarization in dielectrics
## Dipole moment:
$$
P = Qd
$$
## Polarization vector:
$$
\vec P = \epsilon_0 X_e \vec E
$$
where $X_e$ is the electric susceptibility: $X_e = \epsilon_r -1$

## flux density within a dielectric
$$
\vec D = \epsilon \vec R = \epsilon_r \epsilon_0 \vec E = \epsilon_0 \vec E+ \vec P
$$
# Concentrated spheres and coaxial cable
for 2 concentrated spheres of multiple dielectric materials where:
$$
\begin{array}{l}
\epsilon_{r1} : 0 \le \theta \le \theta_1 \\
\epsilon_{r2} : \theta_1 \le \theta \le \pi
\end{array}
$$
we can deduce the following:
$$
|D_1| = |D_{n1}| = \rho_{s1} \quad,\quad |D_2| = |D_{n2}| = \rho_{s2}
$$

$$
\vec{E_{1,2}} = \frac{\rho_{s1,2}}{\epsilon_{r1,2}\,\epsilon_{0}}\vec{a_n}
$$

$$
\psi = \psi_1 + \psi_2 = \left[Q_1 \right]_{r=a} + \left[Q_2 \right]_{r=a} = \left[\int^{2\pi}_0\int^{\theta_1}_0\rho_{s1} \, ds \right]_{r=a} + \left[\int^{2\pi}_0\int^{\theta_1}_0\rho_{s2} \, ds \right]_{r=a}
$$
the normal component $\vec{a_n}$ for the cases:
- **concentrated spheres**: $\vec{a_n} = \vec{a_\theta}$ *spherical coordinates*
- **coaxial cable**: $\vec{a_n} = \vec{a_\phi}$ *cylindrical coordinates*
