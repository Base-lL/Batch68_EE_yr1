# 1.2 Dopants in silicon
## donors
- Group V elements 
- have 5 electrons in the outer orbit
- when substituted in silicon, give an extra electron
-  *N-type silicon*
- Phosphorus (P), Arsenic (As), Antimony (Sb), Bismuth (Bi).
## acceptors
- Group III elements 
- have 3 electrons in the outer orbit
-  *P-type silicon*
- Boron (B), Gallium (Ga), Aluminum (Al), Indium (In).

 donor and acceptor ionization energy is very small (~$50meV$ ) compared to hydrogen ionization energy ($13.6eV$)

# 1.3 Energy band gap

![[Pasted image 20260223222251.png]]

## Valence band
* The band of energy where most of the electrons reside and are involved in the highest energy molecular orbital.
- usually filled with electrons at *insulators* and *semi-conductors* at low temperatures

## Conduction band
- The band energy where *positive* or *negative* mobile charge carriers exist. 
- *Negative* mobile charge carriers are simply electrons that had enough energy to escape the valence band and jump to the conduction band. 
- *Positive* mobile charge carriers are also referred to as **holes**. 
- **Holes** refer to the lack of an electron in the conduction band. In other words, a hole refers to the fact that within the band there is a place where an electron can exist 
- In semiconductors at room temperature, this band is mostly empty, but a few electrons can jump into it.

## DONOR AND ACCEPTOR IN THE BAND MODEL

![[Pasted image 20260223231810.png]]
- $E_d$ is lower because it takes the donor ionization energy (about 50 meV) to free the extra electron from the donor atom into a conduction electron
- $E_c$ is higher because it takes energy for an acceptor atom to receive an extra electron from the valence band, creating a hole 
## Measuring the band gap by light absorption
$E_g$ can be determined from the minimum energy ($hv$) of photons
that are absorbed by the semiconductor.

$$
\text{energy of photon = } hv \;,\quad \text{where: } \quad
\begin{array}{l}
h: \text{plank's constant}\\
v: \text{photon frequency}
\end{array}
$$

to free electrons: $hv \ge E_g$ 
and we know that $v = \dfrac{c}{\lambda}$   
where $c$ is the speed of light and $\lambda$ is the wavelength

we get this:

$$
hv_{min} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda_{max}} \ge E_g
$$

### The electron wave function
it is the solution of the **three-dimensional Schr├Čdinger wave equation**:
$$
-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_0} \nabla^2 \psi + V(r)\psi = E\psi
$$
### Schrodinger wave function:

$$
\nabla^2 \psi = \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial z^2}
$$
### Acceleration

$$
-\frac{q\mathcal{E}}{\hbar^2} \frac{d^2E}{dk^2} = \frac{F}{m}
$$
### Effective mass

$$
\frac{\hbar^2}{d^2E / dk^2}
$$
## 1.5.2 how to measure the effective mass
using **Cyclotron Resonance Technique**

$$
\begin{array}{l}
\dfrac{m_n v^2}{r} = qvB\\
\therefore v = \dfrac{qBr}{m_n}\\
\because v = 2\pi r f\\
\therefore 2\pi r f = \dfrac{qBr}{m_n}
\end{array}
$$
in the end, the effective mass is:
$$
m_n = \dfrac{qBr}{2\pi r f }
$$

where:
- $m_n$ : Effective mass of the electron
- $v$ : Velocity of the electron
- $r$ : Radius of the cyclotron orbit
- $q$ : Charge of the electron
- $B$ : Magnetic flux density
- $f$ : Cyclotron resonance frequency







# 1.6 DENSITY OF STATES
the density states gives the number of available states at an energy E
$$
D_c(E) = \dfrac{\text{number of states in $\Delta E$}}{\Delta E \cdot \text{volume}}
$$

$$
D_c(E) = \frac{8\pi m_n \sqrt{2m_n(E - E_c)}}{h^3} \qquad E \le E_c
$$

$$
D_v(E) \equiv \frac{8\pi m_p \sqrt{2m_p(E_v - E)}}{h^3}  \qquad E \ge E_v
$$

# FERMI FUNCTION 
it is the probability of an energy state being occupied by an electron
![[Pasted image 20260301215815.png]]

$$
 f(E) = \large\frac{1}{1+e^{(E-E_f) / kT}}
$$
where $E_f$ is called the Fermi energy or the Fermi level
by substituting $E = E_f$ we get that the *probability of an electron being in the Fermi energy level is a constant 1/2* for different temperatures and doping 
**Remember:** there is only one Fermi-level in a system at equilibrium.

## Boltzmann approximation:

$$
 f(E) \approx e^{-(E-E_f) / kT} \qquad E-E_f \gg kT
$$

$$
 f(E) \approx 1-e^{-(E_f-E) / kT} \qquad E-E_f\ll kT
$$


# 1.8 Electron and hole
## 1.8.1 Derivation of n and p from D(E) and f(E)

$$
n = \int^\text{top of conduction band}_{E_c} f(E) D_c(E) dE
$$

$$
n = \frac{8\pi m_n \sqrt{2m_n}}{h^3} \int^\infty_{E_c} \sqrt{E-E_c}\;e^{-(E-E_f)/kT} dE
$$
*after long, annoying process of integration that we do not need to know*
$$
\begin{array}{l}
n = N_c e^{-(E_c-E_f)/kT} &\quad,\quad& p = N_v e^{-(E_F-E_v)/kT}\\
N_c = 2 \left( \dfrac{2 \pi\,m_n k T}{h^2} \right)^{3/2} &\quad,\quad& N_v = 2 \left( \dfrac{2 \pi\,m_p k T}{h^2} \right)^{3/2}\\[5px]
\dfrac{N_{c1}}{N_{c2}} = \left(\dfrac{T_1}{T_2}\right)^{3/2} &\quad,\quad& \dfrac{N_{v1}}{N_{v2}} = \left(\dfrac{T_1}{T_2}\right)^{3/2}
\end{array}
$$
- $N_c$ : effective density of states *(of the conduction band)*
- $N_v$ : effective density of states of the valence band
we also get the following equation:
$$
E_c - E_f = kT \ln(\dfrac{N_c}{n}) \quad,\quad E_f - E_c = kT \ln(\dfrac{N_p}{p})
$$

**Remember**: the closer $E_f$ moves up to $E_c$ , the larger n is; the closer $E_f$ moves down to $E_v$ , the larger p is.

# 1.9 General Theory of n and p
charge neutrality: $n + N_a = p + N_d$
and we already know that $np = {n_i}^2$
we can then deduce:
$$
p = \dfrac{N_a - N_d}{2} + \sqrt{ \left( \dfrac{N_a - N_d}{2} \right)^2 + {n_i}^2 }
$$

$$
n = \dfrac{N_d - N_a}{2} + \sqrt{ \left( \dfrac{N_d - N_a}{2} \right)^2 + {n_i}^2 }
$$
let $u = \dfrac{N_a - N_d}{2}$

$$
p = \sqrt{u^2 + {n_i}^2} + u \quad,\quad n = \sqrt{u^2 + {n_i}^2} - u  
$$

for a mostly dopped element:
$$
\begin{array}{c|c}
N_d-N_a \gg n_i \text{ (N-type)} & N_a-N_d \gg n_i \text{ (P-type)} \\
n = N_d - N_a \;,\quad p = \dfrac{{n_i}^2}{n} &
p = N_a - N_d \;,\quad n = \dfrac{{n_i}^2}{p} \\[10px]
\text{if: } N_d \gg N_a: & \text{if: } N_a \gg N_d:\\
n = N_d \;,\quad p = \dfrac{{n_i}^2}{N_d} &
p = N_a \;,\quad n = \dfrac{{n_i}^2}{N_a} 
\end{array}
$$
