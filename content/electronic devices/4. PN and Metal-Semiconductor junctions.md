# 4.1 Building Blocks of the PN Junction and Theory of operation.
the diode is made by doping a P-type material and turning a part of it into N-type. for simplicity now, it's assumed they're both doped by the same amount

![[Pasted image 20260411182000.png]]
there is 3 states for the diode:
### normal state:
- no current is being conducted
### forward bias: 
- the voltage is source terminals connect to the diode's sign
- the inner electrons and holes repel with the connected source, motion then happens
- current is *allowed to pass*
### reverse bias:
- current is conducted opposite to the diode's direction
- the inner electrons and holes attract with the connected source, increasing the depletion layer width
- current is *blocked from passing*

**note**: not all electrons are stopped from moving, some current can still pass through, that's why in graph the reverse bias current is just under zero

## 4.1.2 Built-in Potential

![[Pasted image 20260411195152.png]]

we're interested in finding the built in potential $\phi_{bi}$ 
it's the difference between $qA$ and $qB$, to get a relationship:
- N-region:
$$
n = N_d = N_c\,e^{-qA/kT} \quad\Rightarrow\quad A = \frac{kT}{q} \ln \frac{N_c}{N_d}
$$
- P-region:
$$
n = \frac{{n_i}^2}{N_d} = N_c\,e^{-qB/kT} \quad\Rightarrow\quad B = \frac{kT}{q} \ln \frac{N_c N_a}{{n_i}^2}
$$
subtracting both:
$$
\phi_{bi} = B - A = \frac{kT}{q} \left( \ln \frac{N_c N_a}{n_i^2} - \ln \frac{N_c}{N_d} \right)
$$
**final equation:**
$$
\phi_{bi} = \frac{kT}{q} \ln \frac{N_d N_a}{n_i^2}
$$
## 4.1.3 Poissonâ€™s Equation
$$
\frac{d^2V}{dx^2} = -\frac{dE}{dx} = -\frac{\rho}{\epsilon_s}
$$
where:
- $\epsilon_s$: permittivity
- $\rho$ : charge density
$$
\rho = \;\begin{array}{r} -q N_a & \text{(P-side)} \\[2px] q N_d & \text{(N-side)} \end{array}
$$

# 4.2 Depletion-Layer Model

we will assume that within the depletion region there is a P-side at $x > x_P$ and N-side at $x < -x_N$


## 4.2.1 Field and Potential in the Depletion Layer

 ![[Pasted image 20260411213553.png]]
 
$$
N_a\,|x_p| = N_d\,|x_n|
$$
the more doped side has a shorter width at the depletion layer.
for the shown graph, the 2 areas should be equal.
A one-sided junction is called a $N^+P$ **junction** or $P^+ N$ **junction**

### Electric field
from poisson equation:
$$
\frac{dE}{dx} = -\frac{qN_a}{\epsilon_s}
$$
if we then integrate:
$$
E(x) = -\frac{qN_a}{\epsilon_s}x + C \quad\rightarrow\quad \frac{qN_a}{\epsilon_s}\left( x_p - x \right)
$$
that was for the **P-side**. for the **N-side** where $\rho = q N_d$
$$
E(x) =\frac{qN_d}{\epsilon_s}\left( x- x_n \right)
$$
and this is the integration result
![[Pasted image 20260411214241.png]]



### Voltage
from poisson equation (again), integrating over $x$ (again) from the electric field:
$$
V(x) = 
\begin{array}{r}
\dfrac{q N_a}{2\epsilon_s}(x_P - x)^2 & \text{(P-side)} \\[2px] 
\phi_{bi} -\dfrac{q N_d}{2\epsilon_s}(x - x_N)^2 & \text{(N-side)} 
\end{array}
$$

##  4.2.2 Depletion-Layer Width
if V is continuous at x = 0 

*which generally means the function doesn't disconnect or instantly jump to another value,* $V(0^-) = V(0^+) = V(0)$
*a case for a non continuous function for V is the op-amp without feedback as voltage values jump to the nearest saturation value*

$$
 W_{dep} = x_P - x_N = \sqrt{\frac{2\epsilon_s\, \phi_{bi}}{q} \left( \frac{1}{N_a} + \frac{1}{N_d} \right) }
$$
it can be shortened to
$$
W_{dep} = \sqrt{\frac{2\epsilon_s\, \phi_{bi}}{qN} }
$$
where:
$$
\frac{1}{N} = \frac{1}{N_d} + \frac{1}{N_a}
$$
so $N$ is nearly equal to the lighter dopant density *note that  the small number down is bigger*



# 4.3 Reverse-Biased PN Junction
when in reverse bias, $\phi_{bi}$ gets increased by an equal the connected source $V_r$
![[Pasted image 20260411221958.png]]

the depletion-layer width equation becomes:
$$
W_{dep} = \sqrt{\frac{2\epsilon_s\, (\phi_{bi} + |V_r|)}{qN} }
$$
